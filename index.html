Composition

Basalt forms the dense outer layers from lava flows.

Obsidian, a volcanic glass, appears near vents.

Volcanic ash settles in soft, layered deposits.

Pumice is light and full of air pockets, formed in explosive eruptions.

Each layer marks a different volcanic phase.

Height Range

The mountain rises to a peak of 4,300 meters.

Its height was built gradually by countless eruptions over hundreds of thousands of years.

Lava flows and ash deposits stacked up to form a steep, conical shape.

The visible layers tell the story of its eruptive past.

Location

This volcano sits near a coastal subduction zone, where an oceanic plate sinks beneath a continental plate.

Rivers from the mountain flow rapidly toward the ocean trench nearby.

Its position close to both land and sea makes it geologically active and visually striking.

The environment around it includes steep valleys and dynamic river systems shaped by ice and eruptions.

Status

Mount Koryuna is still active, with eruptions every few hundred years.

It experiences alternating quiet and explosive phases — lava builds it up, while eruptions reshape its slopes.

Seismic activity is regularly monitored, as new magma chambers form deep below.

Though it may seem calm, the volcano remains alive beneath the surface.
